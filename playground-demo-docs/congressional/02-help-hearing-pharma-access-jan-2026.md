## PANEL I: Opening Statements

### [2:03 PM] CHAIRMAN KESSLER:

The HELP Committee will come to order.

Today we examine the pharmaceutical supply chain and the role of intermediaries in driving drug costs for American consumers. This is not a new topic for this committee, but I believe we are at an inflection point. There is growing bipartisan recognition that the current system, in which multiple middlemen extract value between the manufacturer and the patient, is fundamentally broken.

We have a distinguished set of witnesses today, including a colleague from this body who has been at the forefront of this issue. I will keep my opening remarks brief to allow maximum time for testimony and questions.

Senator Delgado, you are recognized for an opening statement.

### [2:06 PM] SENATOR DELGADO:

Thank you, Chairman Kessler.

I come to this hearing today not as a partisan but as a problem-solver. I represent New Mexico, a state where 18.7 percent of adults are uninsured and where prescription drug costs are a kitchen-table issue in every community, from Albuquerque to Las Cruces to the Navajo Nation.

Over the past several months, I have had extensive conversations with colleagues on both sides of the aisle about pharmaceutical pricing reform. What I have found is encouraging. There is more common ground than the cable news coverage would suggest. Republican members I have spoken with, including several on the Finance Committee, share our concern about opaque pricing in the pharmaceutical supply chain, even if they differ on the mechanism of reform.

Today I want to introduce a bipartisan framework that I believe can serve as a foundation for legislation. This framework has three pillars:

**First, PBM transparency.** Pharmacy benefit managers should be required to disclose the spread between what they charge health plans and what they reimburse pharmacies. The current opacity allows PBMs to extract margins that serve neither manufacturers nor patients.

**Second, formulary accountability.** Health plans and PBMs should be prohibited from moving drugs to higher-cost tiers or specialty classifications solely for the purpose of shifting costs to patients when therapeutically equivalent alternatives are not available at the lower tier.

**Third, pass-through pricing.** Rebates negotiated by PBMs on behalf of health plans should be required to flow through to the patient at the point of sale, not retained by the PBM as revenue.

I have shared this framework with Senator Whitfield, who serves on both Finance and this committee, and with Representative Okafor, who chairs the Health Subcommittee on House Energy and Commerce. I believe there is a path to bicameral, bipartisan legislation on at least the first two pillars within this Congress.

I yield back.

### [2:13 PM] CHAIRMAN KESSLER:

Thank you, Senator Delgado. Before we turn to our witness panel, we have a colleague who has asked to testify today on the intersection of PBM reform and drug pricing legislation. Senator Whitfield, who serves on both the Finance Committee and this committee, is recognized.

## TESTIMONY OF SENATOR MARGARET WHITFIELD (D-WA)

### [2:15 PM] SENATOR WHITFIELD:

Thank you, Chairman Kessler, and thank you, Senator Delgado, for your leadership on the bipartisan framework you just described. I want to formally express my support for all three pillars and indicate that I intend to incorporate elements of this framework into companion legislation I am developing on the Finance Committee side.

I asked to testify today because I believe we are making a mistake by treating drug pricing and PBM reform as separate issues. They are two faces of the same coin, and we will not solve one without addressing the other.

Let me explain what I mean with a concrete example. My staff has been working with Dr. Lisa Park, my chief health policy advisor, to map the insulin supply chain from manufacturer to patient. Here is what we found.

A manufacturer sets a list price of $300 for a vial of insulin. The manufacturer then offers a rebate to the PBM, typically 50 to 65 percent, in exchange for favorable formulary placement. The PBM negotiates with the health plan and retains a portion of the rebate as its fee. The health plan sets the patient's cost-sharing based on the list price, not the net price. The patient walks into the pharmacy and pays $300, or whatever their cost-sharing requires, while the PBM has pocketed $40 to $80 in retained rebates and spread pricing on that single transaction.

This is why the Finance Committee is considering the Insulin Price Cap Act, S.1847, as companion legislation to what I hope will emerge from this committee on PBM reform. The price cap addresses the immediate crisis. Patients are dying. Maria Santos, who will testify before the Finance Committee in March, has documented hundreds of cases of insulin rationing. We cannot wait for structural PBM reform to save those patients. But S.1847 alone is not sufficient. Without PBM reform, the underlying incentive structure remains broken, and we will be playing whack-a-mole with one drug category after another.

Senator Delgado's three-pillar framework is precisely the kind of structural reform we need alongside targeted price relief. I have already discussed with the Finance Committee staff how S.1847 and a PBM transparency bill, I am drafting S.892, the PBM Transparency Act, could move in tandem.

I want to address one more point. I know some of my Republican colleagues are wary of S.1847 because they view it as government price-setting. I understand that concern, and I take it seriously. But I would ask them to consider this: the current system is not a free market. When three manufacturers control 90 percent of a life-saving drug's supply, when opaque middlemen extract billions in margins, when patients have zero ability to negotiate or walk away because the alternative is death, that is not a functioning market. S.1847 is not government overreach. It is a correction to a market failure.

I look forward to working with this committee, with Senator Delgado, and with our colleagues in the House to deliver a comprehensive solution.

Thank you.

## PANEL II: Expert Witness Testimony

### [2:26 PM] DR. JAMES CHEN (Brookings Institution):

Thank you, Chairman Kessler. I am a Senior Fellow at the Brookings Institution's Center for Health Policy, where I have spent the past seven years studying pharmaceutical supply chain economics. I appreciate the opportunity to share our analysis with the committee.

Our research has focused specifically on the PBM markup chain, the cumulative value extraction that occurs between a drug's manufacturer and the patient who ultimately purchases it. I want to present three key findings.

**Finding One: The Opacity Premium.** Our analysis of 2024-2025 data from CMS, public filings of the three largest PBMs, and a proprietary dataset of 12.4 million pharmacy claims shows that the average spread between what PBMs charge health plans and what they reimburse pharmacies is 23.7 percent for brand-name drugs and 48.2 percent for generic drugs. For insulin specifically, the spread averaged 31.4 percent. This represents approximately $4.6 billion annually in PBM revenue that is invisible to both health plans and patients.

**Finding Two: Rebate Retention.** Of the $187 billion in manufacturer rebates negotiated by PBMs in 2025, our analysis estimates that PBMs retained approximately $42.8 billion, or 22.9 percent, as fees and retained rebates. The remaining 77.1 percent was passed through to health plans, but crucially, this passthrough rarely reached patients in the form of lower out-of-pocket costs. Instead, it was used to reduce premiums, a diffuse benefit that does not help the individual patient at the pharmacy counter.

**Finding Three: Vertical Integration Amplifies Distortion.** The three largest PBMs, CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx, are now vertically integrated with the three largest health insurers and, in two cases, with major pharmacy chains. This vertical integration creates conflicts of interest that our modeling suggests add 8 to 14 percent to net patient costs compared to a transparent, unconflicted supply chain.

I want to note that Senator Delgado's three-pillar framework directly addresses all three of these findings. Transparency requirements would expose the opacity premium. Formulary accountability would prevent cost-shifting. And pass-through pricing would ensure rebates benefit patients.

I also want to highlight the interaction between PBM reform and the Insulin Price Cap Act that Senator Whitfield referenced. Our modeling suggests that S.1847, combined with meaningful PBM transparency legislation, would reduce insulin out-of-pocket costs by 72 to 85 percent for the average patient, compared to 55 to 64 percent for S.1847 alone. The complementary effect is substantial.

I have submitted detailed written testimony including our methodology, data sources, and sensitivity analyses.

### [2:34 PM] ANGELA MORRISON (Former Express Scripts COO):

Thank you, Chairman. I spent 14 years at Express Scripts, including six years as Chief Operating Officer, and I want to offer an inside perspective that may be uncomfortable for my former colleagues in the PBM industry.

Dr. Chen's numbers are, in my experience, directionally accurate. During my tenure, the internal term we used for retained rebates was "client investment fees," which is a euphemism. The reality is that the PBM business model evolved from a legitimate administrative service into a margin-extraction operation. When I joined Express Scripts in 2008, retained rebates were approximately 8 percent of total negotiated rebates. By 2020, they had reached 24 percent. The vertical integration that Dr. Chen described accelerated this trend dramatically.

I want to be clear about something. PBMs do provide value. They process billions of claims, they negotiate discounts, and they manage formularies. But the current structure, where the same entity negotiates the price, decides which drugs are covered, processes the claim, and in some cases owns the pharmacy, creates irreconcilable conflicts.

I support Senator Delgado's framework, particularly the transparency and pass-through pricing pillars. In my experience, sunlight would be the most effective disinfectant for the practices Dr. Chen has documented.

## PANEL III: Member Questions

### [2:42 PM] SENATOR DELGADO:

Dr. Chen, your finding on the interaction effect between S.1847 and PBM reform is striking. The 72 to 85 percent reduction versus 55 to 64 percent. Can you explain the mechanism behind that additional benefit?

### [2:43 PM] DR. CHEN:

Certainly, Senator. S.1847 caps what the patient pays, but it does not change the underlying supply chain economics. Without PBM reform, the PBM still retains its spread and rebate margins; those costs simply get redistributed elsewhere in the system, typically into higher premiums. With PBM transparency and pass-through pricing, the total system cost decreases because the extracted margins are eliminated. So the cap becomes less costly to subsidize, the health plan savings are greater, and premiums face less upward pressure. It is the difference between capping a symptom and treating the disease.

### [2:45 PM] SENATOR DELGADO:

Thank you. Ms. Morrison, you mentioned your internal term for retained rebates was "client investment fees." Were health plan clients aware of the magnitude of these retained amounts?

### [2:46 PM] ANGELA MORRISON:

In my experience, the largest health plans, those with dedicated pharmacy benefit analysts, had a general sense of the magnitude. But smaller health plans, employer-sponsored plans, and certainly individual consumers had essentially zero visibility. The contracts were structured with confidentiality provisions that prevented disclosure, and the accounting was deliberately complex.

Representative Okafor has indicated he intends to hold a dedicated hearing on PBM transparency in February, and I believe our Brookings analysis will be referenced there as well. The potential for a bicameral, bipartisan approach on this issue is genuinely encouraging.
