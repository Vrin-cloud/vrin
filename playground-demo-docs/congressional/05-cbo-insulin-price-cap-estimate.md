# CONGRESSIONAL BUDGET OFFICE
# COST ESTIMATE

## Insulin Price Cap Act (S.1847)
**As ordered reported by the Senate Committee on Finance on February 27, 2026**

**Publication Date:** March 5, 2026
**Reference Number:** CBO-2026-0312

---

## SUMMARY

S.1847 would cap out-of-pocket costs for insulin products at $35 per month for individuals enrolled in private health insurance, Medicare Part D, and the Federal Employees Health Benefits Program. The bill would also establish a manufacturer discount program for uninsured individuals, requiring insulin manufacturers with annual U.S. insulin revenues exceeding $500 million to provide insulin at no more than $50 per month to eligible uninsured patients.

CBO and the staff of the Joint Committee on Taxation (JCT) estimate that enacting S.1847 would:

- **Reduce the federal deficit by $14.2 billion** over the 2027-2036 period
- **Extend insulin access to approximately 3.2 million additional patients** who currently forgo or ration insulin due to cost
- **Reduce average patient out-of-pocket insulin costs by 62 percent** for commercially insured patients and by 71 percent for uninsured patients

The estimate carries a range of uncertainty. CBO's low estimate is $11.8 billion in deficit reduction and the high estimate is $17.1 billion, depending primarily on manufacturer pricing responses and the rate of patient uptake under the uninsured discount program.

## BASIS OF ESTIMATE

### Affected Population

CBO estimates that approximately 7.4 million Americans currently use insulin, distributed as follows:

- **Medicare beneficiaries:** 3.4 million (already subject to $35 cap under the Inflation Reduction Act; S.1847 extends additional protections)
- **Commercially insured:** 3.1 million (primary beneficiaries of S.1847)
- **Uninsured:** 0.6 million (covered under the manufacturer discount program)
- **Medicaid/CHIP:** 0.3 million (minimal direct impact; indirect effects through manufacturer pricing changes)

CBO projects that S.1847 would result in approximately 3.2 million additional patients maintaining consistent insulin access, primarily comprising:

- 1.9 million commercially insured patients who currently experience cost-related nonadherence (based on American Diabetes Association survey data and the Yale University study on insulin rationing prevalence cited at the March 12, 2026, Senate Finance Committee hearing)
- 0.8 million uninsured patients who would access insulin through the manufacturer discount program
- 0.5 million patients who would transition from emergency/episodic insulin access to consistent prescribed regimens

### Medicare Part D Analysis

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 already established a $35 monthly cap on insulin cost-sharing for Medicare Part D beneficiaries. S.1847 extends this protection in two ways. First, it applies the cap to Medicare Advantage plans that currently impose higher cost-sharing through supplemental benefit structures. Second, it establishes a manufacturer rebate floor of 50 percent for insulin products under Part D, reducing the federal subsidy required to maintain the cap. CBO estimates this rebate floor provision accounts for $6.8 billion of the $10.3 billion in Part D savings over 10 years.

### Manufacturer Impact Analysis

CBO estimates that S.1847 would reduce U.S. insulin revenues for manufacturers by 12 to 18 percent relative to the current-law baseline, depending on the manufacturer and the product mix.

This estimate reflects several offsetting dynamics:

**Revenue-reducing effects:**
- The $35 cap directly reduces the amount that can be collected from commercially insured patients, eliminating the current deductible-phase revenue that constitutes approximately 14 percent of manufacturer insulin revenue.
- The uninsured discount program creates a new below-market distribution channel.
- CBO assumes that the cap will exert indirect downward pressure on manufacturer list prices, as the political and reputational incentive to maintain high list prices diminishes when the cap insulates patients from those prices.

**Revenue-preserving effects:**
- Increased adherence among the 3.2 million additional patients is expected to generate significant volume increases. CBO estimates a 9 to 15 percent increase in total insulin units dispensed.
- Reduced emergency healthcare utilization among the newly adherent population will reduce the uncompensated care burden on the healthcare system, some of which currently falls on manufacturers through the 340B drug pricing program and state Medicaid rebate programs.
- The legislation does not restrict manufacturer pricing for new insulin formulations entering the market after enactment, preserving the innovation incentive for next-generation products.

**Net effect by manufacturer (estimated 10-year revenue impact):**

| Manufacturer | Current U.S. Insulin Revenue (2025) | Estimated Revenue Change |
|---|---|---|
| Lilly | $7.8 billion | -12% to -15% |
| Novo Nordisk | $8.2 billion | -14% to -18% |
| Sanofi | $5.3 billion | -13% to -17% |

CBO notes that all three major insulin manufacturers reported operating margins on insulin products exceeding 40 percent in their most recent public filings. The estimated revenue reductions would reduce these margins but would not, in CBO's assessment, render insulin production unprofitable or create incentives for market exit.

### Interaction with PBM Reform Legislation

CBO was asked to assess the interaction effects between S.1847 and pending PBM transparency legislation, specifically S.892 (PBM Transparency Act, Sen. Whitfield) and the anticipated House Energy and Commerce Committee legislation (Rep. Okafor).

If S.1847 and PBM reform legislation are both enacted:

- **Combined savings increase by approximately $8.5 billion** over 10 years (from $14.2 billion for S.1847 alone to approximately $22.7 billion combined)
- The interaction effect arises because PBM transparency and pass-through pricing requirements would reduce the "hidden cost" that currently supports the rebate-and-spread pricing system. With PBM reform, manufacturer list prices would face market pressure to decline toward net prices, reducing the federal subsidy required to maintain the $35 cap.
- This estimate is consistent with the Brookings Institution analysis by Dr. James Chen, which projected combined savings of $23.4 billion using a different methodology. CBO's lower estimate reflects more conservative assumptions about the pace of PBM behavioral change following transparency mandates.

### Uncertainty and Sensitivity Analysis

CBO's central estimate of $14.2 billion in deficit reduction carries meaningful uncertainty. The primary sources of uncertainty are:

**Market response uncertainty (range: $11.8B to $17.1B).** The largest source of uncertainty is how insulin manufacturers will adjust their pricing and product strategies in response to the cap. CBO's central estimate assumes manufacturers will reduce list prices by 10 to 20 percent over the 10-year window as the cap reduces the incentive for list price escalation. If manufacturers reduce list prices by less than 10 percent, the federal cost of subsidizing the cap increases, reducing net savings to approximately $11.8 billion. If manufacturers reduce list prices by more than 20 percent, net savings increase to approximately $17.1 billion.

**Uptake uncertainty.** The manufacturer discount program for uninsured patients depends on enrollment, which is difficult to predict for a new program. CBO's central estimate assumes 60 percent uptake among eligible uninsured patients by Year 3. If uptake is lower (40 percent), the program costs less but achieves smaller health benefits. If uptake is higher (80 percent), program costs increase but are offset by reduced emergency care spending.

**Innovation effects.** CBO does not model long-term effects on pharmaceutical innovation in its 10-year budget window. However, CBO notes that the $35 cap is set above the current average net price to manufacturers for existing insulin products (approximately $22 to $28 per monthly supply at current manufacturer rebate levels), which suggests that the cap would not reduce manufacturer revenue below current net price levels for most transactions.

## MANDATE EFFECTS

S.1847 would impose private-sector mandates as defined in the Unfunded Mandates Reform Act (UMRA):

- **Manufacturer mandate:** The uninsured discount program requires insulin manufacturers with U.S. revenues exceeding $500 million to provide insulin at below-market prices. CBO estimates the annual cost of this mandate at $340 million to $520 million, exceeding the UMRA threshold.
- **Insurer mandate:** The $35 cap requires health insurers to restructure insulin cost-sharing. CBO estimates the annual cost at $890 million to $1.2 billion, exceeding the UMRA threshold. Insurers are expected to recoup a substantial portion through reduced emergency utilization and manufacturer rebates.

S.1847 would not impose intergovernmental mandates as defined in UMRA.
