## Executive Summary

The legislative landscape for insulin pricing has shifted materially in Q1 2026. The Insulin Price Cap Act (S.1847), introduced by Senator Margaret Whitfield (D-WA) and Senator Elena Delgado (D-NM), passed the Senate HELP Committee on March 20, 2026, by a vote of 16-6, with three Republican members voting in favor. The bill now awaits action by the Senate Finance Committee, where the outcome remains uncertain but trending unfavorable for industry.

The single most significant development is the evolving position of Senator Ryan Nakamura (R-HI), a Finance Committee member whose opposition to S.1847 has been a key element of the industry's defensive strategy. Our intelligence indicates that his office is actively reconsidering its position. This memo provides a detailed risk assessment and strategic recommendations.

## Legislative Status

### S.1847 -- Insulin Price Cap Act

**Current Status:** Passed HELP Committee (amended); pending Finance Committee action.

The HELP Committee markup on March 20, 2026 produced a significantly modified bill through Senator Delgado's bipartisan substitute amendment. The key modifications are:

1. **Phased Implementation:** The $35 copayment cap for insured patients phases in over three years rather than taking effect immediately: Year 1 cap of $75, Year 2 cap of $50, Year 3 and beyond cap of $35. The $75 cap for uninsured patients phases in over two years.

2. **Manufacturer R&D Tax Credit:** A new 15% tax credit for qualifying insulin R&D expenditures, defined as Phase I through Phase III clinical trial costs for novel insulin formulations, delivery systems, and biosimilars. The credit is estimated by the Joint Committee on Taxation to offset approximately $1.8 billion in manufacturer revenue impact over 10 years.

3. **Sunset Clause:** The price cap provisions expire after five years unless reauthorized by Congress. The R&D tax credit and PBM transparency provisions do not sunset.

4. **Small Manufacturer Exemption:** Insulin manufacturers with annual U.S. insulin revenue below $50 million are exempt from the price cap provisions. This amendment was introduced by a Republican member and passed 14-8 with bipartisan support.

The CBO scored the original bill at $14.2 billion in savings over 10 years. A revised score for the HELP-amended version is expected by mid-April and will likely show reduced savings of approximately $10-12 billion due to the R&D tax credit offset and phased implementation.

### Finance Committee Outlook

The Finance Committee has 28 members (15 Democrats, 13 Republicans). A simple majority of 15 votes is required to report the bill favorably. Assuming all 15 Democrats vote yes, the bill advances without Republican support. However, Chairman Alan Richardson (D-OR) is known to prefer bipartisan committee action and has indicated he will seek at least one Republican vote before scheduling a markup.

## Critical Analysis: Senator Nakamura's Shifting Position

Our sources in Senate offices and the lobbying community indicate that Senator Nakamura's position on S.1847 is undergoing a meaningful reassessment. We assess the probability of his supporting the bill (or at minimum, not opposing it) at 35-45%, up from less than 10% in November 2025.

**Factors driving the shift:**

1. **Constituent Pressure:** Nakamura's office reports receiving over 4,800 constituent communications on insulin pricing since January 2026, running approximately 12:1 in favor of price cap legislation. The viral town hall exchange in Maui with Martha Reyes (67, retired schoolteacher paying $1,200/month for insulin for herself and her husband) generated sustained local media coverage in Hawaii.

2. **Community Health Center Visit:** Nakamura's February visit to three Hawaii community health centers, including the Waianae Coast Comprehensive Health Center, was described by a staff member as "genuinely affecting." He met with Dr. James Akana and twelve insulin-dependent patients, including a 14-year-old Type 1 diabetic whose family pays $800 per month out of pocket. Our sources indicate the visit was not a staged political event but was organized through Nakamura's health policy director, Jennifer Walsh, at the senator's personal request.

3. **Staff Dynamics:** Jennifer Walsh, Nakamura's health policy director, is a former CBO analyst who is reportedly sympathetic to the economic case for targeted price interventions in oligopolistic markets. Chief of Staff Michael Torres is more cautious but is focused on Nakamura's re-election positioning in 2030 and recognizes that healthcare affordability is a top-three voter issue in Hawaii.

4. **HELP Committee Markup Observation:** Walsh attended the March 20 HELP Committee markup of S.1847 as an observer. This is an unusual step for Finance Committee staff and strongly suggests that Nakamura's office is conducting detailed due diligence on the bill's amended provisions.

5. **Delgado's Bipartisan Framework:** The phased implementation, R&D tax credit, and sunset clause in Delgado's substitute amendment address several of Nakamura's stated concerns. Our intelligence indicates that Nakamura's office views the amended bill as "significantly more workable" than the original version.

**Factors favoring continued opposition:**

1. **Pharmaceutical Industry Relationships:** Nakamura received $85,000 from the PhRMA PAC and $125,000 total from pharmaceutical industry PACs in the 2024 cycle. PhRMA has maintained intensive engagement with his office, including four meetings in Q4 2025 and his attendance at PhRMA's annual policy dinner on December 3, where he was seated with PhRMA leadership and BioVance CEO Richard Ellsworth.

2. **Free-Market Brand:** Nakamura's political identity is closely tied to free-market economics. Supporting government price caps, even targeted ones, creates ideological tension with his base and could generate a primary challenge from the right in 2030.

3. **Republican Caucus Pressure:** Senate Republican leadership has signaled opposition to S.1847 and is pressuring Finance Committee Republicans to hold the line. A Nakamura defection would be politically damaging for the caucus.

4. **Precedent Concerns:** Nakamura has repeatedly cited the "slippery slope" argument -- that capping insulin prices creates a precedent for government price-setting in other drug categories. This concern remains substantively important to him.

**Our assessment:** The most likely scenario (45% probability) is that Nakamura votes against S.1847 in Finance Committee but does not actively whip against it, allowing it to pass with Callahan's vote. The second most likely scenario (30% probability) is that Nakamura negotiates additional amendments -- likely a broader sunset clause or a higher phase-in cap in Year 1 -- and votes yes. The third scenario (25% probability) is that Nakamura's position reverts to firm opposition following sustained industry engagement.

## Revenue Impact Modeling

If S.1847 passes as amended by the HELP Committee, our financial modeling projects the following impact on BioVance Therapeutics' insulin product portfolio:

**BioVance Insulin Portfolio (2025 Baseline):**
- VanceLog (rapid-acting insulin analog): $2.1 billion U.S. revenue
- VanceGlar (long-acting insulin analog): $1.8 billion U.S. revenue
- VanceMix (premixed insulin): $640 million U.S. revenue
- **Total insulin revenue:** $4.54 billion

**Projected Revenue Impact Under S.1847 (as amended):**
- Year 1 ($75 cap): 8-10% insulin revenue reduction ($363M - $454M)
- Year 2 ($50 cap): 12-15% reduction ($545M - $681M)
- Year 3+ ($35 cap): 15-20% reduction ($681M - $908M)
- **Steady-state annual revenue impact:** $681M - $908M (15-20% reduction)
- **10-year cumulative impact:** $5.8B - $7.6B in foregone revenue

**Mitigating factors:**
- R&D tax credit offsets approximately $45M annually for BioVance
- Volume increases from improved access could offset 3-5% of revenue loss
- International pricing adjustments could partially compensate
- PBM rebate reductions (Title III transparency provisions) could redirect $200-300M annually from PBMs to BioVance's net revenue

## Strategic Recommendations

### Immediate Actions (Next 30 Days)

1. **Voluntary Price Reduction Announcement:** We strongly recommend BioVance announce a voluntary $35/month insulin access program for uninsured patients and a $50/month cap for commercially insured patients through 2027. This action serves multiple purposes: (a) demonstrates good faith that may slow legislative momentum, (b) positions BioVance favorably relative to competitors who have not acted (note: Novo Nordisk announced a voluntary $35 cap in January 2026), (c) provides political cover for Finance Committee members who may prefer voluntary industry action over legislation, and (d) establishes a voluntary framework that BioVance controls rather than a legislative mandate it does not.

2. **Engage Senator Nakamura's Health Policy Staff:** Request a meeting with Jennifer Walsh to present BioVance's voluntary program and discuss BioVance's perspective on the amended bill. Do NOT frame this meeting as opposition to S.1847. Frame it as constructive engagement on implementation details, particularly the small manufacturer exemption, the R&D tax credit scope, and the phase-in timeline. Walsh is analytically minded and will respond better to data than to political arguments.

3. **Support PBM Transparency Provisions:** Publicly endorse Title III of S.1847 (PBM transparency) and H.R.3456 (PBM Accountability Act, Okafor). This signals constructive engagement while redirecting attention toward supply chain intermediaries. PhRMA has adopted a similar strategy, and coordinated industry messaging on PBM reform strengthens the narrative that manufacturers are not the primary problem.

### Medium-Term Actions (Q2 2026)

4. **Finance Committee Engagement:** Focus lobbying resources on the three most persuadable Finance Committee votes: Nakamura (R-HI), Marchetti (R-PA), and Petrov (R-FL). For each, develop customized briefing materials that address their specific concerns -- constituent economics for Nakamura, pharmaceutical employment for Marchetti, and senior access for Petrov.

5. **Coalition Building:** Join or form a manufacturer coalition that supports the "bipartisan framework" elements (phased implementation, R&D credit, sunset) while seeking to increase the Year 1 cap to $100 and extend the phase-in to five years. This "constructive alternative" positioning is more defensible than outright opposition and may influence Finance Committee amendments.

6. **Competitive Intelligence:** Novo Nordisk's voluntary $35 cap program is gaining favorable media coverage and political goodwill. If BioVance does not match, Novo Nordisk will be positioned as the "responsible actor" while BioVance bears disproportionate criticism. We understand from industry sources that Eli Lilly is also considering a voluntary program announcement in Q2.

### Timeline

- **March 2026:** HELP Committee markup complete (done). Revised CBO score expected.
- **April 2026:** Finance Committee staff negotiations on bipartisan amendments.
- **May 2026:** Finance Committee markup likely. Chairman Richardson has indicated a goal of acting before Memorial Day recess.
- **June-July 2026:** Full Senate floor action if both committees report favorably.
- **Fall 2026:** Conference with House legislation (H.R.2901 or H.R.3456) if Senate passes.

## Conclusion

The Insulin Price Cap Act has significant momentum and a plausible path to enactment. Senator Nakamura's evolving position represents both the greatest risk and the greatest opportunity for industry influence. Our recommendation is to pivot from a defensive posture (blocking the bill) to a constructive posture (shaping the bill's final form) while simultaneously pursuing voluntary actions that demonstrate industry responsiveness. The phased implementation and sunset provisions in the HELP-amended bill create a manageable revenue impact in the near term, and voluntary programs can position BioVance to capture market share and political goodwill regardless of the legislative outcome.
