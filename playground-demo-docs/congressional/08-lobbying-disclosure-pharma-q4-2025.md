## Organization Overview

The Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA) is the principal trade association representing the nation's leading biopharmaceutical research companies. As of Q4 2025, PhRMA's membership includes 33 major pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies. The organization's stated mission is to advocate for public policies that encourage the discovery of new medicines and ensure patient access to approved therapies.

PhRMA's government affairs operation is headquartered at 950 F Street NW, Washington, D.C. The organization maintains a lobbying team of 24 in-house lobbyists supplemented by 7 external lobbying firms retained on contract.

## Total Lobbying Expenditures

**Q4 2025 Total:** $8,200,000

This represents a 14% increase over Q3 2025 ($7,190,000) and a 22% increase over Q4 2024 ($6,720,000). The quarterly increase is attributed to intensified legislative activity surrounding the Insulin Price Cap Act (S.1847) and related drug pricing proposals in both chambers of Congress.

**Breakdown by Category:**
- In-house lobbyist compensation and benefits: $3,450,000
- External lobbying firm retainers: $2,180,000

**Full Year 2025 Total:** $29,400,000 (making PhRMA the second-highest lobbying spender in the healthcare sector for the calendar year)

## Congressional Contacts: Senate Finance Committee

PhRMA's lobbying contacts with members of the Senate Finance Committee during Q4 2025 were as follows. All contacts involved in-house lobbyists Robert Cunningham and/or Dr. Patricia Thornton, Vice President of Policy.

### Senator Ryan Nakamura (R-HI)

PhRMA maintained its most intensive engagement with Senator Nakamura during Q4 2025, reflecting his strategic importance as a pivotal vote on the Finance Committee's Health Care Subcommittee.

**Meeting 1 -- October 8, 2025:** Robert Cunningham and Dr. Patricia Thornton met with Nakamura's Health Policy Director, Jennifer Walsh, and Legislative Director Thomas Reeves. The 45-minute meeting focused on PhRMA's analysis of the CBO scoring methodology for S.1847, with PhRMA presenting an alternative economic model suggesting the CBO had underestimated negative impacts on pharmaceutical R&D investment by approximately $4.8 billion over the scoring window. PhRMA provided a 28-page white paper titled "Innovation at Risk: The Hidden Costs of Price Controls."

**Meeting 2 -- October 29, 2025:** Dr. Patricia Thornton met with Senator Nakamura directly for a 30-minute briefing in his Russell Senate Office Building suite. Thornton presented data on insulin pipeline development, arguing that 14 novel insulin formulations currently in Phase II or Phase III clinical trials could be jeopardized by the price cap provisions in S.1847. Thornton also presented PhRMA's counter-proposal: a voluntary manufacturer Patient Affordability Program that would cap out-of-pocket costs at $50/month without legislative mandates.

**Meeting 3 -- November 18, 2025:** Robert Cunningham met with Chief of Staff Michael Torres to discuss the upcoming November Finance Committee hearing on S.1847. Cunningham provided Torres with suggested questions for Nakamura to pose to witnesses, focusing on the international impact of price controls on pharmaceutical investment flows. Torres accepted the document but made no commitments regarding its use.

**Meeting 4 -- December 3, 2025 (PhRMA Annual Policy Dinner):** Senator Nakamura attended PhRMA's annual policy dinner at the Ritz-Carlton Georgetown, along with 8 other members of Congress and approximately 120 pharmaceutical industry executives. Nakamura was seated at the head table with PhRMA President and CEO David Harrington, Dr. Patricia Thornton, and BioVance Therapeutics CEO Richard Ellsworth. During remarks, Harrington praised Nakamura as "a senator who understands that America's pharmaceutical leadership depends on market-based innovation." Nakamura's attendance was reported by STAT News on December 5, 2025.

### Senator Margaret Whitfield (D-WA)

**Meeting 1 -- November 5, 2025:** Dr. Patricia Thornton and Robert Cunningham met with Senator Whitfield and her Legislative Director, Catherine Morales, for a 40-minute meeting requested by PhRMA. The meeting focused on S.1847's price cap provisions. Whitfield pressed Thornton on insulin profit margins, citing data showing that insulin manufacturing costs approximately $3-$6 per vial while list prices exceed $300. Thornton argued that list prices reflect R&D recoupment and do not represent actual transaction prices after rebates. Whitfield described the meeting afterward to reporters as "frank but unproductive."

PhRMA requested three additional meetings with Whitfield's office during Q4 2025. All three requests were declined. Whitfield's office cited scheduling conflicts but also publicly stated that the senator "has heard PhRMA's position and sees no need for further discussion until they are prepared to engage constructively on price caps rather than defending the status quo."

### Senator Elena Delgado (D-NM)

**Meeting 1 -- October 22, 2025:** Robert Cunningham met with Senator Delgado's Health Legislative Assistant, Marcus Rivera, to discuss Delgado's proposed bipartisan substitute amendment to S.1847. PhRMA expressed conditional interest in the phased implementation approach but opposed the fundamental price cap mechanism. Cunningham provided PhRMA's position paper on alternative approaches to insulin affordability.

**Meeting 2 -- December 11, 2025:** Dr. Patricia Thornton met with Senator Delgado directly to discuss a potential bipartisan framework for insulin pricing. Delgado proposed a compromise involving a phased price cap over three years combined with R&D tax credits for insulin manufacturers. Thornton indicated that PhRMA could "potentially support transparency provisions and the R&D tax credit but cannot endorse price caps in any form." Delgado reportedly responded: "Then we have a narrower path to agreement than I'd hoped, but I appreciate the honest conversation."

### Other Senate Finance Committee Members

PhRMA reported contacts with the following additional Finance Committee members during Q4 2025:

- **Sen. Robert Driscoll (R-UT):** 3 meetings. Driscoll remains firmly opposed to S.1847 and has agreed to introduce a Republican alternative focused on PBM reform without price caps.
- **Sen. Thomas Callahan (R-OH):** 2 meetings. Callahan is considered a potential swing vote. He has expressed interest in the insulin-specific provisions but opposes broader price control precedent.
- **Sen. Diane Park (D-CA):** 1 meeting. Park is a co-sponsor of S.1847 and expressed continued strong support for price caps.
- **Sen. James Okonkwo (D-IL):** 2 meetings. Okonkwo raised concerns about impact on pharmaceutical employment in Illinois, where 14,000 people work in pharmaceutical manufacturing.

## Key Lobbyists

**Robert Cunningham** -- Senior Vice President, Government Affairs. Former senior counsel on the Senate Finance Committee under Chairman Orrin Hatch (2015-2019). Cunningham's prior Finance Committee service provides him with extensive relationships among current committee staff. He is PhRMA's primary point of contact for Finance Committee engagement on drug pricing legislation. Lobbying compensation: $485,000 (2025 annual).

**Dr. Patricia Thornton** -- Vice President of Policy. PhD in Health Economics from Johns Hopkins. Former senior economist at the Congressional Budget Office (2010-2016), where she worked on pharmaceutical cost modeling. Thornton leads PhRMA's policy research operation and serves as the organization's principal spokesperson on drug pricing economics. She testified before the Senate Finance Committee in November 2025 against the price cap provisions of S.1847, arguing that price controls would reduce industry R&D spending by $12 billion annually and jeopardize 14 insulin formulations currently in clinical development. Lobbying compensation: $410,000 (2025 annual).

**Laura Beckwith** -- Director of Congressional Affairs. Former legislative assistant to Rep. Chidi Okafor (R-GA) on the House Energy and Commerce Committee (2019-2022). Beckwith serves as PhRMA's primary House liaison and has been central to coordination between PhRMA's Senate strategy on S.1847 and House companion legislation, particularly H.R.3456 (PBM Accountability Act) sponsored by her former boss. Lobbying compensation: $285,000 (2025 annual).

## Strategic Priorities (Internal Memorandum Excerpt)

The following is excerpted from PhRMA's Q4 2025 Government Affairs Strategic Priorities memorandum, which was disclosed as part of the lobbying filing:

> **Priority 1 -- Senate Finance Committee:** Maintain Senator Nakamura's opposition to the price cap provisions of S.1847. Nakamura's vote is essential to preventing bipartisan momentum on the Finance Committee. Our intelligence indicates that constituent pressure in Hawaii is increasing, and his office has been receptive to our economic arguments but is also seeking political cover. We recommend continued high-touch engagement, including arranging meetings between the senator and pharmaceutical executives who can speak to R&D pipeline risks. Secondary priority: Reinforce Senator Driscoll's opposition and cultivate Senator Callahan as a "no" vote or abstention.

> **Priority 2 -- Bipartisan Alternative:** If price cap legislation cannot be blocked, ensure any bipartisan compromise includes: (a) voluntary rather than mandatory price caps, (b) R&D tax credits to offset revenue impacts, (c) phased implementation over no fewer than 3 years, and (d) a sunset clause requiring congressional reauthorization. Senator Delgado's substitute amendment framework partially aligns with these objectives and may serve as a negotiating baseline.

> **Priority 3 -- House Strategy:** Support Rep. Okafor's H.R.3456 PBM Accountability Act as the preferred legislative vehicle. PBM reform redirects political energy toward intermediaries rather than manufacturers. Laura Beckwith's relationship with the Okafor office positions us well to influence the bill's development. If PBM reform advances in the House, it creates a viable conference negotiation path that could dilute or replace Senate price cap provisions.

> **Priority 4 -- Public Narrative:** Continue investment in direct-to-consumer advertising campaign ("Innovation Saves Lives") highlighting pharmaceutical breakthroughs enabled by market-based pricing. Q4 media spend: $14.2 million across television, digital, and print. Polling indicates the campaign has shifted public perception by 4 points in favor of "innovation protection" versus "price regulation" framing.
