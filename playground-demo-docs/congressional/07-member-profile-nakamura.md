# Member Profile: Senator Ryan Nakamura (R-HI)

**Compiled by:** Congressional Research Service, Legislative Reference Division

## Personal and Professional Background

Senator Ryan Kenji Nakamura (Republican, Hawaii) was born on September 14, 1971, in Honolulu, Hawaii. He is a third-generation Japanese American whose grandparents immigrated to Hawaii in the 1930s and worked on sugar plantations on the Big Island. Nakamura graduated magna cum laude from Stanford University in 1993 with a degree in Economics and subsequently earned his MBA from the Wharton School of Business at the University of Pennsylvania in 1997.

Following his MBA, Nakamura joined Goldman Sachs in New York City as an analyst in the Healthcare Investment Banking division. Over the course of eleven years at the firm, he rose to the position of Vice President, specializing in mergers and acquisitions for pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies. During his tenure at Goldman Sachs, Nakamura advised on over $18 billion in healthcare transactions, including the $4.2 billion Meridian Pharmaceuticals-Axion BioSciences merger in 2005 and the $2.8 billion IPO of Pacific Therapeutics in 2007.

In 2008, Nakamura returned to Hawaii and entered public service. He was elected to the Hawaii State House of Representatives in 2010, representing District 25 (Kailua-Waimanalo). He served two terms in the state legislature, where he chaired the House Committee on Economic Development and focused on tax reform, small business incentives, and tourism industry regulation. He authored the Hawaii Innovation Economy Act of 2013, which created tax credits for biotechnology startups operating in the state.

Nakamura was elected to the United States Senate in 2018, defeating incumbent Senator Alan Fujimoto in a closely contested Republican primary before winning the general election with 52.3% of the vote against Democratic challenger State Senator Lisa Kamaka. He was re-elected in 2024 with 54.7% of the vote, running on a platform of fiscal responsibility, military readiness, and free-market healthcare reform.

## Legislative Record on Healthcare

Senator Nakamura's voting record on healthcare issues reflects a broadly free-market orientation with selective pragmatic departures. Key votes include:

- **Affordable Care Act Repeal (2019):** Voted YES on the Health Care Freedom Act, which would have repealed the individual mandate and rolled back Medicaid expansion. The measure failed 49-51.
- **Generic Drug Competition Act (2020):** Voted YES on S.1324, which streamlined FDA approval pathways for generic drugs and prohibited pay-for-delay agreements between brand-name and generic manufacturers. The bill passed 78-22 and was signed into law.
- **Prescription Drug Importation Amendment (2021):** Voted NO on an amendment that would have permitted importation of prescription drugs from Canada and other approved countries. Nakamura argued the proposal posed safety risks and undermined domestic pharmaceutical innovation.
- **Insulin Price Cap Act (S.1847, 2025-2026):** Currently uncommitted. Nakamura has not co-sponsored the bill but has participated actively in Finance Committee hearings on the topic. His position has evolved notably between June 2025 and March 2026.

## Position Evolution on Drug Pricing

Senator Nakamura's stance on drug pricing regulation has undergone a notable shift over the past year, which has attracted significant attention from both the pharmaceutical industry and patient advocacy organizations.

**June 2025 — Senate Finance Committee Hearing on Prescription Drug Costs:** During the committee's hearing on patient perspectives, Nakamura delivered what was widely characterized as his most forceful statement against government intervention in drug pricing. He stated: "Government has no business setting prices in a free market. Every time Washington interferes with pricing, innovation suffers. The United States leads the world in pharmaceutical breakthroughs precisely because we allow the market to function." He challenged a patient witness who testified about rationing insulin due to cost, responding that "the solution is more competition, not more regulation" and characterized price caps as "European-style rationing that would cripple the pipeline of new treatments."

**November 2025 — Senate Finance Committee Hearing on S.1847:** During testimony from Dr. Patricia Thornton, Vice President of Policy at PhRMA, Nakamura gave another forceful speech against government price caps. He endorsed Thornton's argument that price controls would reduce pharmaceutical R&D investment by an estimated $12 billion annually. He stated: "Dr. Thornton's analysis confirms what the data has always shown. Price caps are a short-term political fix with devastating long-term consequences for patients who need tomorrow's cures." He questioned the Congressional Budget Office's methodology for scoring S.1847, suggesting the CBO had underestimated the negative impact on pharmaceutical innovation investment.

**January 2026 — Town Hall in Maui:** During a constituent town hall in Kahului, Nakamura was confronted by Martha Reyes, a 67-year-old retired schoolteacher who described spending $1,200 per month on insulin for herself and her husband. Video of the exchange went viral on social media, accumulating over 4.2 million views. Nakamura's response -- "I hear you, and I'm looking at this very carefully" -- was a marked departure from his earlier dismissive tone.

**February 2026 — Visit to Hawaii Community Health Centers:** Nakamura visited three community health centers on Oahu and the Big Island over the Presidents' Day recess. At the Waianae Coast Comprehensive Health Center, he met with Dr. James Akana, who described patients skipping insulin doses and diluting medication to stretch supplies. Nakamura also met with twelve insulin-dependent patients, including a 14-year-old Type 1 diabetic whose family was paying $800 per month out of pocket. After the visit, Nakamura released a statement saying the experience "was deeply affecting and has given me a great deal to consider as we evaluate legislative options."

**March 2026 — Interview with Honolulu Star-Advertiser:** In a March 12, 2026 interview, Nakamura stated: "I remain a free-market conservative, but I'm open to targeted approaches that address specific market failures. The constituent letters I've received have given me pause, and I believe there may be room for bipartisan action that protects patients without undermining the innovation ecosystem. I'm in close consultation with my colleagues on the Finance Committee about what that might look like." This statement was widely interpreted as signaling a potential shift from outright opposition to conditional openness on insulin pricing legislation.

## Political Analysis

Senator Nakamura occupies a strategically significant position in the drug pricing debate. His seat on the Finance Committee, combined with his evolving stance, makes him one of the most closely watched votes on S.1847. The pharmaceutical industry, through PhRMA and individual company PACs, has invested substantially in maintaining his opposition. At the same time, constituent pressure in Hawaii, where healthcare costs are a top-three voter concern, appears to be pulling him toward engagement with bipartisan solutions.

His background in pharmaceutical investment banking gives him both credibility on market dynamics and potential vulnerability to charges of industry bias. Patient advocacy groups, including the American Diabetes Association and T1International, have specifically targeted Nakamura in grassroots campaigns urging him to support S.1847.

The key question for the 119th Congress is whether Nakamura's rhetorical shift translates into legislative action. His vote on the Finance Committee could determine whether S.1847 advances to the Senate floor with bipartisan support.
