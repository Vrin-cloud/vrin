## PANEL I: Witness Testimony

### [10:16 AM] COMMISSIONER RACHEL HUANG (Federal Trade Commission):

Thank you, Chairman Okafor and members of the subcommittee. I am here today to present interim findings from the Federal Trade Commission's investigation into pharmacy benefit manager business practices, which we initiated in June 2024.

The FTC issued orders under Section 6(b) of the FTC Act to the six largest PBMs, which collectively manage pharmacy benefits for approximately 94 percent of all insured Americans. We compelled production of internal documents, financial records, and contract terms covering the period 2018 to 2025. I want to share three principal findings.

**Finding One: Spread Pricing.** Spread pricing is the practice of charging health plans more for a drug than the PBM reimburses the dispensing pharmacy. The PBM retains the difference. Our investigation found that spread pricing is pervasive and substantial.

Across the six PBMs, the average spread for brand-name drugs was 18.3 percent of the plan-paid amount. For generic drugs, the average spread was 42.7 percent. For specialty drugs, including insulin, the average spread was 27.6 percent.

In dollar terms, we estimate that the six largest PBMs collected approximately $7.3 billion in spread pricing revenue in 2025 alone. This is revenue that is invisible to health plans under most current contract structures. It does not appear as a line item. It is embedded in the difference between two numbers that the PBM controls on both sides.

**Finding Two: Rebate Retention and Manipulation.** PBMs negotiate rebates from manufacturers in exchange for favorable formulary placement. The ostensible purpose is to reduce costs for the health plan and, ultimately, the patient. However, our investigation found that the rebate system has been co-opted.

Internal documents from multiple PBMs show explicit strategies to maximize retained rebates at the expense of patient cost. One document, which I will quote directly from our public report, stated: "Formulary placement should prioritize products with highest net revenue contribution, incorporating both retained rebates and administrative fees." This is a direct admission that drug coverage decisions are driven by PBM revenue, not patient cost or clinical value.

We found that PBMs retained an average of 24.1 percent of negotiated rebates across all drug categories. For insulin specifically, retained rebates averaged 28.3 percent, representing approximately $1.9 billion annually. Internal modeling documents showed that some PBMs deliberately steered patients toward higher-list-price insulin products because those products generated larger absolute rebates, even when lower-cost alternatives were available.

**Finding Three: Spread Pricing Markup by Drug Category.** I want to present specific data on the categories where PBM markups are most extreme, because this directly relates to the drug pricing debate happening in both chambers.

For insulin products specifically, our investigation found that PBMs add an average markup of 40 to 60 percent between what they charge health plans and the combination of manufacturer net price and pharmacy reimbursement. In some cases, the PBM margin on a single insulin transaction exceeded the pharmacy's dispensing margin by a factor of twelve.

This matters because the debate over insulin pricing often focuses exclusively on manufacturer list prices. And manufacturer prices are indeed a problem. But our data shows that even if manufacturers reduced list prices by 30 percent, PBM spread pricing and rebate retention practices would absorb a significant portion of those savings before they reached patients.

I have submitted the FTC's interim report for the record. The full report, including enforcement recommendations, will be published in the second quarter of 2026.

### [10:27 AM] GARY BURDETTE (Independent Pharmacist):

Chairman Okafor, Ranking Member Williams, thank you for having me here. I own Burdette Family Pharmacy in Parkersburg, West Virginia. My father opened it in 1978. I took it over in 2003.

I am here because PBM practices are killing independent pharmacies, and when we close, our communities lose more than a drugstore. They lose the healthcare provider who knows their name, who notices when they stop picking up their medication, who calls their doctor when something does not look right.

In 2020, there were approximately 21,000 independent pharmacies in the United States. Today there are fewer than 16,800. That is a loss of 4,200 pharmacies in five years, predominantly in rural and underserved communities. In West Virginia alone, we have lost 47 independent pharmacies since 2020.

The mechanism is simple. PBMs reimburse us below our cost of acquisition for an increasing number of drugs. For certain insulin products, the PBM reimburses me $232 for a product that costs me $261 to purchase from the wholesaler. I lose $29 every time I fill that prescription. But I am contractually required to fill it under the PBM network agreement. If I refuse, I lose access to the network and all of its patients.

Meanwhile, the same PBM is charging the health plan $347 for that same prescription. The PBM is making $115 on a transaction where I am losing $29.

I am not asking for subsidies. I am not asking for special treatment. I am asking for transparency. If the PBM's contract with the health plan were visible, if the plan could see that the PBM is charging $347 and paying me $232, the plan would demand a better deal. That is how markets are supposed to work.

Commissioner Huang's data on the 40 to 60 percent markup for insulin is consistent with what I see in my pharmacy every day. And I want to connect this to the broader drug pricing debate. Senator Whitfield's Insulin Price Cap Act would help patients at the counter, and I support it. But if we do not fix the PBM problem, the cap will be funded by someone, either the health plan, the employer, or the taxpayer, while the PBM continues to extract its margin. PBM reform and drug price reform have to go together.

### [10:35 AM] DR. JAMES CHEN (Brookings Institution):

Thank you, Chairman Okafor. I testified before the Senate HELP Committee last month on this same topic, and I want to build on that testimony with additional analysis specific to the House legislative framework.

I want to make three points specific to the legislation this subcommittee is developing.

First, the Brookings analysis I presented to the HELP Committee found PBM spread pricing averaging 23.7 percent for brand drugs and 48.2 percent for generics. Commissioner Huang's FTC data, which is based on compelled internal records rather than our estimates from public data, shows 18.3 percent and 42.7 percent respectively. The FTC's numbers are slightly lower, which gives me confidence that our estimates were conservative rather than inflated. The bottom line is the same: PBMs are extracting billions in margins that are invisible to health plans and patients.

Second, the interaction effects between PBM reform and the Insulin Price Cap Act are substantial. Our updated modeling, incorporating the FTC data, suggests that the combined effect of S.1847 and comprehensive PBM transparency legislation would save the healthcare system approximately $23.4 billion over ten years, roughly $8 billion more than either reform alone. Senator Whitfield has been right to insist that these reforms move together.

Third, I want to highlight a finding from our analysis of Senator Delgado's three-pillar framework that is directly relevant to Chairman Okafor's legislative approach. The pass-through pricing pillar, requiring that manufacturer rebates flow to patients at the point of sale, is the single most impactful reform for patients. Our modeling shows it would reduce average patient out-of-pocket costs by 31 percent across all drug categories, with insulin patients seeing reductions of 38 to 47 percent. If this subcommittee can move pass-through pricing legislation, it would be transformative.

## PANEL II: Member Questions

### [10:48 AM] CHAIRMAN OKAFOR:

Commissioner Huang, I want to return to the 40 to 60 percent markup figure for insulin. Can you walk us through a specific example of how a PBM markup works on an insulin transaction?

### [10:49 AM] COMMISSIONER HUANG:

Certainly, Chairman. I will use a composite example drawn from our data. A manufacturer sets a list price of $300 for a vial of insulin. The manufacturer has agreed to a 55 percent rebate with the PBM, so the manufacturer's net received price is $135. The PBM reimburses the dispensing pharmacy $228, which is the pharmacy's acquisition cost of $261 minus the $33 "direct and indirect remuneration," or DIR, fee that the PBM claws back after the fact. So the pharmacy actually receives $228 on a $261 cost, losing $33. Meanwhile, the PBM charges the health plan $310, which is the list price plus a small administrative fee. The PBM's total take on this single transaction is: $310 from the plan, minus $228 to the pharmacy, minus $135 to the manufacturer, plus $33 in DIR fees. That is $310 minus $228 minus $135 plus $33, which equals a PBM margin of approximately $80, or roughly 52 percent of the manufacturer's net price.

### [10:52 AM] CHAIRMAN OKAFOR:

Fifty-two percent. The PBM's margin is 52 percent of what the manufacturer actually receives, and the pharmacy is losing money. That is an extraordinary finding, Commissioner.

### [10:53 AM] REPRESENTATIVE MARTINEZ (R-TX):

Commissioner Huang, has the FTC identified any antitrust concerns related to the vertical integration of PBMs with health insurers and pharmacy chains?

### [10:54 AM] COMMISSIONER HUANG:

Representative Martinez, our investigation has identified what we characterize as significant competitive concerns related to vertical integration. When the same corporate entity owns the PBM, the health insurer, and the pharmacy, there are inherent conflicts of interest and opportunities for self-preferencing that disadvantage independent pharmacies and competing health plans. We are still developing our enforcement recommendations, but I expect the full report will include specific structural and behavioral remedies for the Commission's consideration.

### [10:56 AM] REPRESENTATIVE JACKSON (D-IL):

Mr. Burdette, you mentioned that you lose $29 on certain insulin prescriptions but are contractually required to fill them. What happens if you simply refuse?

### [10:57 AM] GARY BURDETTE:

Representative, if I refuse to fill a prescription that the PBM contract requires me to fill, the PBM can terminate my network participation. That means every patient who has insurance managed by that PBM can no longer use my pharmacy. In Parkersburg, one PBM manages benefits for approximately 40 percent of my customer base. Losing that network would close my doors within six months.

### [10:58 AM] CHAIRMAN OKAFOR:

I want to close the questioning by making a commitment to this subcommittee and to the American people. We will introduce bipartisan PBM transparency legislation within 60 days of this hearing. That legislation will require disclosure of spread pricing, mandate pass-through of rebates to patients at the point of sale, prohibit below-cost pharmacy reimbursement, and ban the practice of DIR fee clawbacks.

This legislation will be coordinated with the Senate HELP Committee's efforts and with Senator Whitfield's PBM Transparency Act, S.892. Our goal is to deliver a bill to the President's desk in this Congress. The American people have waited long enough.
