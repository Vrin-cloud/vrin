# Senate Finance Committee Hearing: "Prescription Drug Costs: Patient Perspectives"

**Date:** June 15, 2025
**Time:** 10:00 AM -- 1:15 PM
**Location:** 215 Dirksen Senate Office Building, Washington, D.C.
**Committee:** Senate Committee on Finance
**Presiding:** Chairman Alan Richardson (D-OR)

---

## Opening Statements

### Chairman Alan Richardson (D-OR)

Chairman Richardson called the hearing to order at 10:03 AM and delivered his opening remarks:

"Good morning. This committee convenes today to hear directly from Americans about the real-world impact of prescription drug costs on their lives, their health, and their families. We will hear from patients, physicians, and leading economists about a crisis that touches every community in this country. Americans pay more for prescription drugs than citizens of any other developed nation. In 2024, total U.S. prescription drug spending exceeded $405 billion -- an increase of 8.3% over the prior year. For insulin alone, U.S. patients pay an average of $98.70 per unit, compared to $8.81 in Australia, $12.00 in Canada, and $7.52 in the United Kingdom. These numbers are not abstract. They represent families choosing between medication and groceries, between insulin and rent. This committee has a responsibility to understand why American patients bear this burden and what legislative remedies are available."

### Ranking Member Sen. Robert Driscoll (R-UT)

"Thank you, Mr. Chairman. I share the concern for patients facing high drug costs. However, I want to ensure this hearing examines the full picture -- including the role of pharmacy benefit managers, insurance plan design, and the regulatory burdens that drive up costs. The United States leads the world in pharmaceutical innovation. In 2024, 73% of all novel drug approvals worldwide originated from U.S.-based research. That leadership is not an accident. It is the product of a market-based system that rewards risk-taking and investment. Any legislative response to drug pricing must preserve that innovation engine. I look forward to hearing from our witnesses."

### Senator Margaret Whitfield (D-WA)

"Thank you, Chairman Richardson. I want to start with a number: 1.3 million. That is the estimated number of Americans who ration their insulin because they cannot afford the full prescribed dose. I have met with dozens of these patients in Washington state. I have sat across the table from mothers who dilute their children's insulin to make it last longer. I have spoken with seniors who skip doses and hope for the best. This is not a policy abstraction. This is a public health emergency, and it demands a legislative response. I look forward to introducing legislation later this session that addresses insulin affordability directly, and I hope my colleagues on both sides of the aisle will engage with the substance of these proposals."

### Senator Ryan Nakamura (R-HI)

Senator Nakamura delivered his opening statement, which was subsequently characterized by media outlets as his strongest public statement against government intervention in drug pricing:

"Thank you, Mr. Chairman. I appreciate this committee's attention to healthcare costs, which are a concern for families in Hawaii and across the country. However, I want to be direct about where I stand. Government has no business setting prices in a free market. Every time Washington interferes with pricing, innovation suffers. The United States leads the world in pharmaceutical breakthroughs precisely because we allow the market to function. We develop 65% of the world's new chemical entities. We invest $102 billion annually in pharmaceutical R&D -- more than the next five countries combined. That is not a coincidence. That is the direct result of a pricing system that rewards innovation.

"I understand that drug costs are a burden for many Americans. But the answer is not to impose European-style rationing on the most productive pharmaceutical ecosystem in human history. The answer is more competition, faster generic approvals, and transparency in the supply chain -- particularly from pharmacy benefit managers, who extract billions in hidden rebates while patients pay full price at the counter. I hope today's hearing will examine the full supply chain rather than focusing exclusively on manufacturers, and I caution my colleagues against the seductive simplicity of price caps that would ultimately cost patients far more than they save."

## Panel I: Patient Testimony

### Witness 1: Maria Gonzalez, Phoenix, Arizona

Maria Gonzalez, age 42, is a Type 1 diabetic who has been insulin-dependent since age 9. She is a single mother of two children and works as a dental hygienist. Her employer-sponsored insurance plan carries a $3,000 annual deductible and a 30% coinsurance requirement for specialty medications.

**Key testimony excerpts:**

"My monthly insulin costs, before I hit my deductible, are approximately $1,400 for two vials of long-acting insulin and one vial of rapid-acting insulin. That is $16,800 per year, just for insulin -- not counting test strips, pump supplies, or endocrinologist visits. My annual salary is $52,000. After taxes, housing, and basic expenses, I have about $800 per month of discretionary income. In January, February, and March -- before I meet my deductible -- I am spending nearly my entire discretionary income on insulin.

"I have rationed insulin. I am not proud of that, but I am being honest with this committee. In February of this year, I stretched a 30-day supply of long-acting insulin to 40 days by reducing my dose. I ended up in the emergency room with diabetic ketoacidosis. The ER visit cost $14,200. My insurance covered most of it, but my out-of-pocket share was $2,800. The entire crisis could have been avoided if my insulin had been affordable in the first place.

"I did not choose to have Type 1 diabetes. I was nine years old. And I will need insulin every day for the rest of my life. It is not a luxury. It is not optional. It is the difference between living and dying."

### Witness 2: Harold Chen, Seattle, Washington

Harold Chen, age 71, is a retired Boeing engineer on Medicare Part D. He uses two types of insulin for Type 2 diabetes diagnosed in 2008.

**Key testimony excerpts:**

"In 2024, my out-of-pocket costs for insulin under Medicare Part D were $3,840. I am in the coverage gap -- the so-called donut hole -- for four months of the year, during which my costs spike dramatically. My monthly Social Security income is $2,100. During those four months, I am spending 45% of my income on insulin alone.

"I served this country as an engineer for 35 years. I paid into Medicare my entire working life. And now the system tells me that the insulin I need to survive will cost me nearly half my retirement income for a third of the year. That is not the deal I was promised."

Senator Whitfield, during her questioning, noted that Chen resides in her state and represents "the thousands of Washingtonians I hear from every month about this exact crisis."

### Witness 3: James Whitaker, Atlanta, Georgia

James Whitaker, age 58, is uninsured and self-employed as an electrician. He was diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes in 2015 and requires daily insulin injections.

**Key testimony excerpts:**

"I do not have health insurance. I looked at the marketplace plans, and the cheapest option with drug coverage was $680 per month with a $5,000 deductible. I cannot afford that. So I pay cash for my insulin. Last month, I paid $892 for a 90-day supply at Costco -- and that is the cheapest pharmacy I could find.

"I have tried every manufacturer discount program. Most of them require insurance. The ones that don't have income limits that I just barely exceed because they count gross business revenue, not my actual take-home. I fall through every crack in the system.

"I ration my insulin. I take 80% of my prescribed dose and hope my blood sugar stays manageable. My endocrinologist knows and tells me it is dangerous, but she also knows I cannot afford the full dose. Last year my A1C was 9.2 -- well above the target of 7.0. My doctor told me at this rate, I am looking at kidney damage within five years."

## Panel I: Questioning

### Exchange Between Senator Nakamura and Maria Gonzalez

This exchange was widely reported in media coverage of the hearing:

**Nakamura:** "Ms. Gonzalez, I appreciate your testimony. Let me ask -- have you explored the manufacturer patient assistance programs? Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi all offer programs that can bring insulin costs down significantly."

**Gonzalez:** "Senator, I have applied to three different programs. Two of them rejected me because my employer-sponsored insurance disqualifies me, even though my insurance barely covers anything until I hit my deductible. The third program offered me a $50 discount on a $700 monthly cost. With respect, Senator, these programs are designed for press releases, not for patients."

**Nakamura:** "I understand your frustration. But the solution to high insurance deductibles is insurance reform, not price controls on the companies that develop these life-saving drugs. If we cap the price of insulin, what signal does that send to the companies investing billions in the next generation of diabetes treatments? I am looking at a pipeline of 14 novel insulin formulations in clinical trials right now. Price caps are European-style rationing, and they would put every one of those innovations at risk."

**Gonzalez:** "Senator, I hear your concern about innovation. But I need insulin today. Not in ten years when those 14 formulations might come to market. Today. And today I cannot afford it. Insulin was discovered in 1921. It is over 100 years old. The R&D costs were recouped decades ago. What I am paying for is not innovation -- it is profit."

**Nakamura:** "Well, I think the economics are more complicated than that, but I appreciate your perspective and I do hear your concern."

The exchange was covered by the Washington Post, New York Times, STAT News, and multiple healthcare trade publications. Patient advocacy groups including T1International cited Nakamura's "European-style rationing" characterization as evidence of what they called "the disconnect between Congress and the patients it serves."

### Exchange Between Senator Whitfield and James Whitaker

**Whitfield:** "Mr. Whitaker, you mentioned your A1C is 9.2 and your doctor has warned about kidney damage. Can you walk us through what that means in practical terms?"

**Whitaker:** "If my kidneys fail, I need dialysis. Dialysis costs about $90,000 per year, most of which would be covered by Medicare since kidney failure qualifies you automatically. So the system won't pay $300 a month to keep me healthy, but it will pay $7,500 a month to keep me alive once I'm sick. That does not make sense to anyone I've talked to -- including my Republican neighbors."

**Whitfield:** "The irony you describe is precisely why insulin affordability is not just a moral issue but a fiscal issue. The CBO has estimated that every dollar invested in insulin access for uninsured patients saves $3.40 in downstream emergency and chronic care costs over a ten-year window."

## Panel II: Expert Testimony

### Dr. Catherine Morales, Health Economist, Brookings Institution

Dr. Morales presented international pricing comparison data that became a central point of reference in subsequent legislative debate.

**Key findings presented:**

- U.S. insulin prices are 2.5 times the average of other OECD nations when adjusted for purchasing power parity
- The average annual cost of insulin per patient in the U.S. was $6,200 in 2024, compared to $1,400 in Canada, $980 in Germany, and $620 in the United Kingdom
- Between 2012 and 2024, the average list price of insulin in the U.S. increased by 287%, while the Consumer Price Index rose by 34% over the same period
- Three manufacturers -- Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi -- control 96% of the global insulin market, creating an oligopolistic pricing environment
- The gross profit margin on insulin for the three major manufacturers averages 72%, compared to an average gross profit margin of 55% for all pharmaceutical products

### Dr. Raymond Sato, Economist, Heritage Foundation

Dr. Sato presented the counter-argument, focusing on R&D investment dynamics:

"The international price comparison is misleading because it compares apples and oranges. Other countries free-ride on American pharmaceutical innovation. They impose price controls and consume drugs developed with American investment. If the U.S. adopts similar controls, there is no other market to absorb the R&D costs. Global pharmaceutical innovation will decline.

"Our modeling suggests that a $35 insulin copayment cap, if paired with manufacturer price constraints, would reduce insulin-specific R&D investment by $2.8 billion annually and total pharmaceutical R&D by $12 billion annually due to portfolio reallocation effects. That translates to approximately 30 fewer novel drug approvals per decade."

### Exchange Between Senator Nakamura and Dr. Sato

**Nakamura:** "Dr. Sato, your research confirms what I have long believed. Can you elaborate on the portfolio reallocation effect you mentioned?"

**Sato:** "Certainly, Senator. When you reduce the expected return on one therapeutic category, companies shift R&D dollars to other categories where returns remain uncapped. This is basic capital allocation theory. Insulin R&D would decline first, but the signal to the broader market is that any drug category could be next. That chilling effect on investment is the real long-term cost of price controls."

**Nakamura:** "This is exactly the point. We are not just talking about insulin. We are talking about the precedent. Once government starts setting prices for one drug, the political pressure to expand that to other drugs becomes irresistible. It is a slippery slope that ends with government-run pharmaceutical pricing, and that is a world where American pharmaceutical leadership disappears."

Senator Whitfield interjected: "I would note for the record that Dr. Sato's $12 billion figure has been challenged by the CBO, which estimates the R&D impact at $1.8 to $3.4 billion annually -- roughly one-quarter of Dr. Sato's projection. I would also note that insulin was discovered over 100 years ago, and the 'innovation' we are protecting amounts largely to incremental reformulations designed to extend patent protection rather than deliver meaningful clinical improvements. The three major insulin analogs on the market today -- Humalog, Novolog, and Lantus -- have been available for over 20 years."

## Hearing Conclusion

Chairman Richardson concluded the hearing at 1:15 PM, noting that the testimony would inform legislative development in the committee. He indicated that the Finance Committee would hold a follow-up hearing later in the fall focused on specific legislative proposals, including anticipated legislation from Senator Whitfield.

Senator Whitfield stated to reporters after the hearing that she intended to introduce the Insulin Price Cap Act within 90 days, with the goal of committee action before the end of the first session of the 119th Congress.

Senator Nakamura told reporters: "This was a productive hearing. I heard compelling testimony from patients, and I take their concerns seriously. But I remain convinced that market-based solutions -- generic competition, PBM transparency, and insurance reform -- are the right approach. Government price-setting is not the answer."
