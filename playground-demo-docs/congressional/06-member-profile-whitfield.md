## KEY LEGISLATION

### S.1847 — Insulin Price Cap Act (Lead Sponsor)
- **Introduced:** January 28, 2026
- **Cosponsors:** 14 (9 Democrats, 3 Independents, 2 Republicans as of March 2026)
- **Status:** Ordered reported by Senate Finance Committee on February 27, 2026; CBO cost estimate published March 5, 2026
- **Provisions:**
  - Caps out-of-pocket insulin costs at $35/month for all insured Americans (extending Medicare IRA cap to commercial market)
  - Establishes manufacturer discount program for uninsured patients ($50/month cap)
  - Applies to all FDA-approved insulin products including analog, biosimilar, and over-the-counter formulations
  - Requires manufacturer rebate floor of 50% under Medicare Part D
- **CBO score:** $14.2 billion deficit reduction over 10 years (range: $11.8B-$17.1B)
- **Projected impact:** 3.2 million additional patients maintaining consistent insulin access
- **Opposition:** PhRMA (testified against at March 12 hearing via Dr. Patricia Thornton, citing R&D investment concerns and $2.1B average drug development cost)
- **Bipartisan dynamics:** Sen. Ryan Nakamura (R-HI), initially a strong opponent of government price caps (see November 2025 hearing statement), has signaled openness to negotiation after receiving over 4,200 constituent communications on insulin costs. Publicly stated willingness to "explore a bipartisan path" at the March 2026 Finance Committee hearing.

### S.892 — PBM Transparency Act (Lead Sponsor)
- **Introduced:** February 14, 2026
- **Cosponsors:** 8 (5 Democrats, 1 Independent, 2 Republicans as of March 2026)
- **Status:** Referred to Senate HELP Committee; expected markup Q2 2026
- **Provisions:**
  - Requires PBM disclosure of spread pricing amounts to health plans
  - Mandates pass-through of manufacturer rebates to patients at point of sale
  - Prohibits below-cost pharmacy reimbursement
  - Bans retroactive DIR (direct and indirect remuneration) fee clawbacks
  - Establishes FTC enforcement authority over PBM business practices
- **Legislative coordination:** Aligned with Sen. Elena Delgado's (D-NM) three-pillar bipartisan framework (transparency, formulary accountability, pass-through pricing) introduced at the January 2026 HELP Committee hearing. Coordinated with Rep. Chidi Okafor's (R-GA) House Energy and Commerce Health Subcommittee legislation for bicameral compatibility.
- **CBO interaction:** When combined with S.1847, projected to generate $22.7 billion in total healthcare system savings over 10 years (per CBO interaction analysis)

## PROFESSIONAL BACKGROUND: HEALTHCARE POLICY

Senator Whitfield's healthcare policy expertise predates her Senate career by more than two decades.

**Washington State Department of Health (2008-2017).** As Director, Whitfield oversaw a state health department with 7,800 employees and a $2.3 billion biennial budget. Key accomplishments during her tenure include:
- Implementation of the state's Medicaid expansion under the Affordable Care Act, enrolling 620,000 previously uninsured Washingtonians
- Creation of the Washington Prescription Drug Affordability Board, one of the first state-level bodies to review drug pricing
- Development of a statewide diabetes prevention program that reduced Type 2 diabetes incidence by 11 percent over five years
- Negotiation of a state supplemental rebate agreement with insulin manufacturers that reduced the state's Medicaid insulin costs by 34 percent

**Washington State Health Care Authority (2003-2008).** As Deputy Director, Whitfield managed the state's employee health benefits program and the state pharmaceutical purchasing pool. She led the creation of a multi-state purchasing consortium with Oregon, Nevada, and Montana that achieved 22 percent average savings on commonly prescribed drugs.

**King County Public Health Department (1993-2003).** Whitfield began her public health career as a policy analyst focused on chronic disease management in low-income communities. Her analysis of diabetes care disparities in South King County communities led to targeted clinic funding that became a model for the state's subsequent diabetes prevention program.
