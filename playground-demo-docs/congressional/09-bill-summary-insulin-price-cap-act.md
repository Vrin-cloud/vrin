## Key Provisions (As Introduced)

### Title I -- Insulin Copayment Cap for Insured Patients

**Section 101:** Establishes a maximum monthly copayment of $35 for insulin products for patients enrolled in:
- Employer-sponsored health insurance plans
- Individual market plans (including Affordable Care Act marketplace plans)
- Medicare Part D prescription drug plans
- Medicare Advantage plans with prescription drug coverage
- TRICARE and Federal Employees Health Benefits Program (FEHBP)
- Medicaid managed care plans

**Section 102:** Requires health insurance issuers to apply the $35 copayment cap beginning with plan years starting on or after January 1, 2027. The cap applies to a 30-day supply of any insulin product approved or licensed by the FDA, regardless of formulation (rapid-acting, long-acting, intermediate, premixed, or biosimilar).

**Section 103:** Prohibits issuers from increasing premiums, deductibles, or other cost-sharing for non-insulin products to offset the revenue impact of the insulin copayment cap. The Secretary of Health and Human Services is directed to establish a monitoring program to detect and penalize such cost-shifting practices.

### Title II -- Insulin Affordability Program for Uninsured Patients

**Section 201:** Establishes the Insulin Affordability Program, requiring manufacturers of insulin products with annual U.S. revenue exceeding $100 million to offer a discount program to uninsured patients. The maximum out-of-pocket cost under the program is $75 per month for a 30-day supply of insulin.

**Section 202:** Manufacturers must make the program available at all retail pharmacies and through mail-order pharmacy channels. Patients qualify by self-attesting to uninsured status; manufacturers may verify eligibility but may not require documentation beyond a signed attestation form.

**Section 203:** The Secretary of HHS shall establish an online portal (InsulinHelp.gov) where uninsured patients can determine eligibility and locate participating pharmacies. The portal must be operational within 18 months of enactment.

### Title III -- PBM Transparency Requirements

**Section 301:** Requires pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) operating in the insulin supply chain to submit annual reports to the Secretary of HHS disclosing:
- Aggregate rebates received from insulin manufacturers
- Aggregate rebates passed through to health plan sponsors
- Administrative fees and spread pricing revenue attributable to insulin products
- The percentage of manufacturer rebates retained by the PBM versus passed through to plan sponsors and patients

**Section 302:** The Secretary shall publish an annual summary of PBM transparency reports, aggregated to protect proprietary information but sufficient to allow Congress and the public to evaluate the insulin supply chain.

**Section 303:** PBMs found to have materially misrepresented information in their transparency reports shall be subject to civil penalties of up to $100,000 per violation, as determined by the Secretary.

### Title IV -- Manufacturer Price Justification

**Section 401:** Manufacturers of insulin products must submit annual reports to the Secretary of HHS justifying any increase in the wholesale acquisition cost (WAC) of insulin products exceeding the rate of medical inflation (as measured by the Consumer Price Index for Medical Care, CPI-M). Reports must include:
- Total R&D expenditures for insulin products in the preceding fiscal year
- Revenue and unit sales for each insulin product
- A detailed justification for any price increase, including the relationship between the price increase and R&D costs, manufacturing costs, and clinical improvements
- Executive compensation for the five highest-paid officers of the manufacturer

**Section 402:** Manufacturers that fail to submit the required price justification report, or that submit reports determined by the Secretary to be materially incomplete, shall be subject to civil penalties of up to $50,000 per day of non-compliance.

### Title V -- Enforcement and Penalties

**Section 501:** Establishes a civil penalty of up to $50,000 per violation for manufacturers, issuers, or PBMs that fail to comply with any provision of the Act. The Secretary of HHS is authorized to assess penalties after notice and opportunity for a hearing.

**Section 502:** State attorneys general are authorized to bring civil actions in federal court to enforce the provisions of the Act on behalf of residents of their states.

**Section 503:** The Secretary shall submit an annual report to Congress on compliance with the Act, including the number and nature of violations detected, penalties assessed, and the impact of the Act on insulin pricing and patient access.

## CBO Score

The Congressional Budget Office released its cost estimate for S.1847 on March 5, 2026.

**Key findings:**
- **Federal savings:** $14.2 billion over 10 years (FY2027-FY2036), primarily from reduced Medicare Part D and Medicaid expenditures on insulin
- **Medicare Part D savings:** $9.8 billion over 10 years
- **Medicaid savings:** $3.1 billion over 10 years
- **Administrative costs:** $620 million over 10 years for HHS implementation, portal development, and enforcement
- **Revenue impact:** $1.3 billion increase in tax revenue over 10 years due to the R&D tax credit provision in the HELP Committee substitute amendment (which reduces manufacturer deductions, partially offset by new credit)
- **Impact on private insurance premiums:** CBO estimates a negligible impact (less than 0.1%) on average premiums, as the insulin copayment cap shifts costs within the existing insurance risk pool rather than creating new obligations

CBO noted that the $14.2 billion savings estimate does not account for potential behavioral changes by manufacturers, such as voluntary price reductions that might occur in anticipation of or in response to the legislation.

## Committee Action

### Senate HELP Committee

The HELP Committee held two hearings on S.1847:
- **October 8, 2025:** "The Insulin Affordability Crisis: Patient and Provider Perspectives" -- testimony from endocrinologists, patients, and hospital administrators
- **January 22, 2026:** "S.1847 -- Policy Analysis and Implementation Considerations" -- testimony from CBO analysts, HHS officials, and health economists

**Markup -- March 20, 2026:** The HELP Committee conducted a full markup of S.1847. Senator Delgado introduced a bipartisan substitute amendment that modified the bill to include a three-year phase-in period for the copayment cap, a manufacturer R&D tax credit, and a five-year sunset clause requiring congressional reauthorization. The substitute amendment passed 15-7 with bipartisan support. The bill as amended passed the committee 16-6. Three Republican committee members voted in favor: Senators Hoffman (R-ME), Moreau (R-LA), and Henderson (R-AK).

### Senate Finance Committee

The Finance Committee held two hearings related to S.1847:
- **June 15, 2025:** "Prescription Drug Costs: Patient Perspectives" -- the first major hearing on drug pricing in the 119th Congress, featuring notable exchanges between Senator Nakamura and patient witnesses
- **November 19, 2025:** "S.1847 and the Future of Insulin Pricing" -- testimony from Dr. Patricia Thornton (PhRMA), patient advocates, and economists

The Finance Committee has not yet scheduled a markup. Sources indicate that Committee Chair Senator Alan Richardson (D-OR) is seeking to secure at least one additional Republican vote before proceeding. Senator Nakamura's evolving position is considered central to this calculation.

## Related Legislation

- **S.892 -- PBM Transparency Act:** Introduced by Senator Whitfield (D-WA). Focuses exclusively on PBM disclosure requirements. Overlaps with Title III of S.1847 but includes additional provisions on rebate pass-through mandates. Referred to Finance Committee.

- **H.R.3456 -- PBM Accountability Act:** Introduced by Representative Chidi Okafor (R-GA) in the House Energy and Commerce Committee. Addresses PBM reform from a Republican perspective, emphasizing market transparency over price controls. PhRMA has expressed support for H.R.3456 as a preferred alternative to S.1847's price cap approach.

- **H.R.2901 -- Insulin Access for All Act:** Introduced by Representative Sandra Nguyen (D-WA). House companion to S.1847 with substantially similar provisions. Referred to House Energy and Commerce Committee and House Ways and Means Committee.

## Stakeholder Positions

**In Support:**
- American Diabetes Association (ADA): "S.1847 represents the most significant legislative effort to address insulin affordability in a generation. We urge swift passage."
- JDRF (Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation): "The $35 copayment cap would provide immediate relief to the 8.4 million Americans who depend on insulin."
- T1International: "No one should die because they cannot afford insulin. S.1847 is a lifeline."
- AARP: "Seniors on Medicare are among the hardest hit by insulin costs. This bill delivers real savings."
- Endocrine Society: "Evidence-based policy that balances patient access with continued pharmaceutical innovation."

**In Opposition:**
- PhRMA: "While we share the goal of improving insulin affordability, government-imposed price caps are the wrong approach. Price controls will reduce R&D investment and ultimately harm patients who need next-generation insulin therapies. We support transparency reforms and voluntary manufacturer programs." (Statement by Dr. Patricia Thornton, VP of Policy, November 19, 2025)
- BioVance Therapeutics: "S.1847 fails to address the root cause of high insulin costs -- the PBM rebate system that siphons value away from patients. We support H.R.3456 as a more effective and market-based solution." (Statement, October 2025)
- National Association of Manufacturers: Opposed on grounds that the price justification reporting requirements in Title IV constitute government overreach into private business operations.

**Mixed/Conditional Support:**
- Novo Nordisk: Has not taken a public position on S.1847 but announced a voluntary $35 insulin cap program in January 2026, which supporters of the bill characterized as evidence that the legislation is already having its intended effect.
- America's Health Insurance Plans (AHIP): Supports the insulin copayment cap but opposes provisions that could shift costs from manufacturers to insurers.
