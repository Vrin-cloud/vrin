## PANEL I: Opening Statements

### [10:03 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

The Senate Finance Committee will come to order. Good morning, and thank you all for being here.

Today's hearing examines the broad landscape of rising healthcare costs and their impact on American families. Healthcare spending in the United States reached $4.8 trillion in 2024, representing 17.6 percent of GDP. For the average American family, healthcare costs, including premiums, out-of-pocket expenses, and taxes directed toward public programs, now exceed $28,000 per year.

I convened this hearing because the Finance Committee has jurisdiction over Medicare, Medicaid, and the tax code provisions that shape our healthcare system, and I believe we have both the authority and the responsibility to act.

I want to signal to this committee that I am developing legislation focused specifically on prescription drug pricing. The details are not yet final, and I look forward to input from colleagues on both sides of the aisle, but I believe we need targeted action on the drugs that are causing the most acute financial hardship for patients. Insulin, in particular, is an area where I believe bipartisan agreement is achievable. I intend to introduce specific legislation early next year, and today's testimony will help shape that effort.

Senator Nakamura, you are recognized for your opening statement.

### [10:09 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

Thank you, Chairwoman Whitfield.

I appreciate the Chairwoman convening this hearing, and I share her concern about the impact of healthcare costs on American families. Where I suspect we will differ, respectfully, is on the solution.

Let me be direct. I have deep reservations about the direction the Chairwoman has signaled regarding prescription drug price legislation. Government-imposed price caps are, in my view, a fundamental violation of free-market principles that have served this country well. They have been tried in other countries, and the results are instructive: reduced pharmaceutical innovation, longer wait times for new therapies, and the offshoring of research and development to countries that still respect market incentives.

The United States accounts for approximately 44 percent of global pharmaceutical R&D spending. That is not an accident. It is a direct result of a market environment that rewards innovation. When we tell pharmaceutical companies that the government will determine what they can charge for their products, we are telling the next generation of researchers that their breakthroughs will be valued at whatever a political process decides, not what the market determines.

I understand that drug costs are high. I understand that families are struggling. But the solution is not to replace market signals with government mandates. The solution is to increase competition, streamline FDA approval processes for generic and biosimilar drugs, and address the anti-competitive practices that prevent market forces from working as they should.

I urge my colleagues to resist the siren song of price caps and instead pursue reforms that harness the power of competition. I look forward to a vigorous and honest debate.

I yield back.

### [10:16 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

Thank you, Senator Nakamura. I welcome the debate, and I think the evidence our witnesses will present today will speak to the limitations of a purely competition-based approach in a market that has significant structural barriers to entry.

Our witness panel today consists of:

## PANEL II: Witness Testimony

### [10:20 AM] DR. KATHERINE VOGEL (Government Accountability Office):

Thank you, Chairwoman Whitfield and members of the committee. The GAO has conducted extensive analysis of prescription drug pricing trends at the request of this committee, and I am here to present our findings.

Our report, GAO-26-104, examined pricing trends for the 50 most commonly prescribed drugs in the United States over the period 2010 to 2025. I want to highlight several key findings.

**Insulin pricing.** The average wholesale acquisition cost for a standard vial of insulin has increased from approximately $35 in 2010 to $300 in 2025, representing a nominal increase of 757 percent. Adjusted for inflation, the increase is 584 percent. This trend is particularly striking because insulin is not a new drug. The underlying molecule has been available for over a century. Even the most recent analog insulin formulations have been on the market for 15 to 20 years, meaning that development costs have been substantially amortized.

The GAO identified three primary drivers of this price trajectory. First, limited manufacturer competition. Despite the age of the product, the insulin market remains highly concentrated, with three manufacturers, Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi, controlling approximately 90 percent of global supply. Biosimilar insulin products, which were expected to introduce price competition, have faced significant market entry barriers, including formulary exclusion by PBMs and patent thickets filed by incumbent manufacturers.

Second, rebate-driven list price escalation. Our analysis found a strong correlation, r-squared of 0.87, between the growth in PBM-negotiated rebates and the growth in manufacturer list prices over the 2010 to 2025 period. In essence, manufacturers raise list prices to fund the rebates that PBMs demand for formulary access. The net price to the manufacturer has remained relatively stable, but the list price, which determines patient cost-sharing, has skyrocketed.

Third, the absence of reference pricing. Unlike most OECD countries, the United States does not employ external or internal reference pricing for pharmaceuticals. Our comparison of insulin prices across 14 OECD nations found that U.S. list prices are 3.4 to 8.7 times higher than the median OECD price for equivalent products.

**Broader prescription drug trends.** Beyond insulin, our analysis found that 39 of the 50 most commonly prescribed brand-name drugs experienced list price increases exceeding the rate of inflation over the past five years. The median annual list price increase was 7.8 percent, compared to a general inflation rate of 3.2 percent over the same period.

I want to note one additional finding that is relevant to the policy discussion. The GAO examined the 12 countries that have implemented some form of drug price regulation or negotiation. In 9 of those 12 countries, pharmaceutical R&D investment as a share of GDP either held steady or increased in the five years following implementation. The evidence does not support the claim that price regulation necessarily reduces innovation, although the relationship is complex and context-dependent.

I have submitted our full report for the record.

### [10:29 AM] MICHAEL BRANNIGAN (Kaiser Family Foundation):

Thank you, Chairwoman. I want to add consumer-level data to Dr. Vogel's macro analysis.

The Kaiser Family Foundation conducts an annual Health Tracking Poll, and our 2025 results are sobering. Twenty-nine percent of American adults reported not filling a prescription in the past year due to cost. Among adults with diabetes, that figure rises to 37 percent. Among adults with household incomes below $40,000, it is 43 percent.

We also track what we call the "impossible choice" metric: the percentage of Americans who report choosing between healthcare costs and other basic necessities. In 2025, 22 percent of adults reported choosing between medication and food. Fourteen percent reported choosing between medication and housing costs. These are not hypothetical scenarios. These are real decisions being made by millions of Americans every month.

For insulin specifically, our tracking data shows that the average out-of-pocket cost for a 30-day insulin supply increased from $47 in 2018 to $112 in 2025 for commercially insured patients. For uninsured patients, the average out-of-pocket cost is $387 per month.

I want to note that these cost barriers have real clinical consequences. A 2024 study in the New England Journal of Medicine estimated that cost-related insulin nonadherence results in approximately 3,400 preventable deaths and $7.2 billion in avoidable emergency healthcare costs annually.

## PANEL III: Member Questions

### [10:42 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

Dr. Vogel, I want to engage with your finding on the 12 countries that implemented price regulation. You said R&D investment held steady or increased in 9 of 12 countries. But those countries were not the primary source of pharmaceutical innovation to begin with. The United States is. Is it not possible that those countries maintained R&D investment precisely because they were free-riding on the innovation funded by the U.S. market?

### [10:43 AM] DR. VOGEL:

Senator, that is a legitimate analytical question, and I want to be transparent about the limitations of our cross-country comparison. The "free-rider" hypothesis is debated among economists, and our data does not definitively resolve it. What I can say is that the five countries in our sample that are significant pharmaceutical innovators, the United Kingdom, Germany, Switzerland, Japan, and South Korea, all showed stable or increased R&D investment after implementing pricing reforms. However, none of those countries implemented reforms as aggressive as a hard price cap, and the counterfactual, what would have happened without reform, is inherently uncertain.

### [10:45 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

I appreciate that honest answer. And let me be equally honest. I am not here to defend pharmaceutical companies charging $300 for a vial of insulin. That is difficult to justify to my constituents or to anyone. What I am here to defend is the principle that markets, not governments, should set prices. If we can find a way to make the insulin market actually function as a competitive market, I believe prices would come down without government intervention.

Dr. Vogel, your report mentions biosimilar market entry barriers. Can you elaborate on what specific barriers exist?

### [10:47 AM] DR. VOGEL:

Certainly. We identified four primary barriers. First, patent thickets: the three incumbent insulin manufacturers hold a combined 247 active patents related to insulin products, formulations, delivery devices, and manufacturing processes. These create a minefield for biosimilar developers. Second, PBM formulary exclusion: even when biosimilar insulins receive FDA approval, PBMs have in several documented cases excluded them from preferred formularies in exchange for larger rebates from incumbent manufacturers. Third, manufacturing complexity: analog insulin is biologically complex to manufacture, and facility investment requirements create significant capital barriers. Fourth, regulatory pathway uncertainty: the FDA's biosimilar approval pathway, while improved, still involves longer timelines and higher costs than traditional generic drug approvals.

### [10:50 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

That is very helpful. Those are the kinds of barriers I believe we should be focused on dismantling. If we can address patent thickets, prevent anti-competitive PBM practices, and streamline the biosimilar approval pathway, we might achieve the same price reductions the Chairwoman is pursuing without government price caps.

### [10:51 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

I appreciate Senator Nakamura's engagement on the competition angle, and I want to note that I agree with him that competition reforms are important. However, I want to ask Mr. Brannigan a question. If we pursue only competition-based reforms, what is the realistic timeline before patients see relief at the pharmacy counter?

### [10:52 AM] MICHAEL BRANNIGAN:

Chairwoman, based on our analysis of previous biosimilar market entries and the timeline for the reforms Senator Nakamura described, a realistic estimate for meaningful price impact through competition alone would be five to eight years. Patent reform requires legislation, litigation, and resolution. Biosimilar development and approval takes three to five years. Market penetration sufficient to drive price reductions takes an additional two to three years after approval. During that five to eight year period, based on current trends, we would expect insulin list prices to increase an additional 25 to 40 percent.

### [10:54 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

Five to eight years. During which time, if the New England Journal estimates are correct, approximately 17,000 to 27,000 additional Americans will die from insulin rationing.

Mr. Brannigan, is there a bridge measure that could provide immediate relief while we work on structural competition reforms?

### [10:55 AM] MICHAEL BRANNIGAN:

Chairwoman, a price cap or out-of-pocket cap, such as what you have described for your forthcoming legislation, would provide the most immediate relief. The Inflation Reduction Act's $35 cap for Medicare beneficiaries, enacted in 2022, has been effective for that population. Extending a similar cap to the commercially insured and uninsured populations would address the gap. Whether that takes the form of a manufacturer price cap, an out-of-pocket cap, or a combination is a policy design question, but the speed-to-relief argument favors direct intervention over waiting for competition to materialize.

### [10:57 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

I hear the urgency argument, and I do not dismiss it lightly. I have constituents who are struggling right now. But I want to caution this committee against legislating in haste on an issue with significant long-term consequences for innovation. I will continue to engage with the Chairwoman's office on this, and I reserve my position on any specific legislation until I see the text.

### [10:58 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

I appreciate that, Senator Nakamura. And I want to acknowledge that your points about competition reform are well-taken. The legislation I am developing will include competition provisions alongside the price cap. I believe we need both, and I hope to earn your support when the bill is ready.
