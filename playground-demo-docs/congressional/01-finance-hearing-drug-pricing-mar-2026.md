# UNITED STATES SENATE
# COMMITTEE ON FINANCE
## Hearing: "Lowering Drug Prices: The Insulin Crisis"

**Date:** March 12, 2026
**Time:** 10:00 AM EDT
**Location:** 215 Dirksen Senate Office Building, Washington, D.C.
**Presiding:** Senator Margaret Whitfield (D-WA), Chair

## PANEL I: Opening Statements

### [10:02 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

The Senate Finance Committee will come to order.

Today we convene to examine one of the most pressing healthcare crises facing American families: the skyrocketing cost of insulin. I want to thank my colleagues for their attendance and our witnesses for traveling to be here today.

Let me begin with a simple fact that should trouble every member of this committee. In 2010, a single vial of insulin cost approximately $35. Today, that same vial costs over $300. That is not a typo. That is not inflation. That is a 757 percent increase in the price of a drug that was discovered over a century ago, a drug that millions of Americans need to survive.

I introduced the Insulin Price Cap Act, Senate Bill 1847, because I believe no American should choose between insulin and groceries. No parent should have to ration their child's medication because the pharmacy bill exceeds their rent. And yet, that is exactly what is happening in communities across this country, from rural towns in my home state of Washington to urban centers in every state represented on this committee.

S.1847 would cap the out-of-pocket cost of insulin at $35 per month for all insured Americans and establish a manufacturer discount program for the uninsured. The Congressional Budget Office has scored this legislation, and I look forward to discussing those numbers today.

Before I turn to my distinguished ranking member, I want to note that this hearing builds on work this committee began last November, when we examined the broader landscape of rising healthcare costs. The testimony we heard then planted the seeds for S.1847, and I believe today's hearing will demonstrate why this legislation is not just good policy but urgent policy.

Senator Nakamura, you are recognized for an opening statement.

### [10:08 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

Thank you, Chairwoman Whitfield.

I want to be candid with this committee and with the American people. I have historically been skeptical of government-imposed price caps on pharmaceutical products. I have spoken at length about the risks of distorting market incentives and undermining the research and development pipeline that has made American pharmaceutical innovation the envy of the world.

I still hold those concerns. They are not trivial.

However, I must also be honest about what I am hearing from my constituents in Hawaii. Over the past several months, my office has received over 4,200 letters, emails, and phone calls from families struggling with insulin costs. I met with a retired teacher in Honolulu last month, Mrs. Akiko Tanaka, who showed me her pharmacy receipts. She is spending $847 per month on insulin for herself and her husband. That is more than their mortgage payment.

So while I remain cautious about the mechanism of price caps, I am not here today to dismiss the problem. I am here to listen. I want to understand from our witnesses whether S.1847 is the right tool, whether there are market-based alternatives that could achieve the same result, and what the unintended consequences might be.

I yield back.

### [10:14 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

Thank you, Senator Nakamura. I appreciate your candor, and I know your constituents do as well.

I will now introduce our witness panel. Testifying today are:

- **Dr. Patricia Thornton**, Executive Vice President for Policy and Research, Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America, PhRMA
- **Maria Santos**, insulin-dependent patient and founder of the Insulin Affordability Coalition
- **Dr. Robert Kimball**, Director of Pharmaceutical Economics, Georgetown University Health Policy Institute

## PANEL II: Witness Testimony

### [10:17 AM] DR. PATRICIA THORNTON (PhRMA):

Thank you, Chairwoman Whitfield, Ranking Member Nakamura, and distinguished members of the committee. I appreciate the opportunity to present the pharmaceutical industry's perspective on this critical issue.

Let me begin by acknowledging what we all agree on: no patient should face barriers to accessing the insulin they need. PhRMA and its member companies share that goal.

However, I must respectfully urge this committee to consider the full picture before advancing S.1847. The average cost of developing a new drug in the United States is $2.1 billion. That figure, validated by the Tufts Center for the Study of Drug Development and updated in their 2025 analysis, accounts for the cost of failed compounds, clinical trials, regulatory compliance, and post-market surveillance.

Insulin is not a static product. The insulin available today is dramatically different from the insulin discovered by Banting and Best in 1921. Modern analog insulins, including rapid-acting, long-acting, and ultra-long-acting formulations, represent billions of dollars in cumulative research investment. These formulations have meaningfully improved patient outcomes, reduced hypoglycemic events by up to 40 percent, and enabled more flexible dosing regimens.

Price caps, as proposed in S.1847, risk several unintended consequences. First, they compress the revenue available to fund next-generation insulin development, including glucose-responsive "smart" insulins currently in Phase II trials. Second, they create a precedent for government price-setting that could extend to other therapeutic areas, chilling venture capital investment across the biotech sector. Third, and most importantly, they do not address the root cause of high out-of-pocket costs, which is the opaque and distortionary role of pharmacy benefit managers, or PBMs, in the pharmaceutical supply chain.

I have submitted detailed written testimony that includes data on insulin R&D investment trends, a comparison of international price regulation outcomes, and our alternative proposal for expanded patient assistance programs.

Thank you.

### [10:23 AM] MARIA SANTOS:

Chairwoman Whitfield, Ranking Member Nakamura, members of the committee, my name is Maria Santos. I am 34 years old. I was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes when I was nine.

I am not an economist. I am not a lobbyist. I am a middle school teacher in Albuquerque, New Mexico, and I am here because last July I almost died.

My insurance changed in January of last year. My new plan had a $1,200 annual deductible for specialty drugs, and my insulin was reclassified as a specialty drug. Until I met that deductible, I was paying full price: $327 per vial, and I use approximately three vials per month.

By July, I had fallen behind. I started rationing. Instead of the prescribed dose, I was taking two-thirds. Then half. On July 14th, I went into diabetic ketoacidosis and was hospitalized for four days. The hospital bill was $23,000.

I am one of the 7.4 million Americans who use insulin. The American Diabetes Association estimates that one in four of us has rationed our medication due to cost. That is not a policy debate. That is a public health emergency.

Dr. Thornton mentioned $2.1 billion in drug development costs. I want to be respectful, but the original insulin patent was sold for $1 by its discoverers because they believed it was unconscionable to profit from a life-saving discovery. The three companies that control 90 percent of the global insulin market, Eli Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and Sanofi, reported combined insulin revenues of $21.3 billion in 2025. They are not struggling.

Please pass S.1847. People are dying.

Thank you.

## PANEL III: Member Questions

### [10:30 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

Thank you, Ms. Santos. I want to note for the record that the experience Ms. Santos described, rationing insulin due to cost, is not an outlier. A 2025 Yale University study published in the Annals of Internal Medicine found that 26.4 percent of insulin users reported cost-related nonadherence in the prior 12 months.

Dr. Thornton, I have a question. PhRMA's written testimony argues that PBMs are the primary driver of high out-of-pocket costs. The Senate HELP Committee held a hearing on this exact subject in January, and I testified there about the connection between PBM practices and drug pricing. I agree that PBMs are part of the problem. But my question is this: if Eli Lilly can sell insulin in Canada for $42 per vial and in Germany for $57 per vial, why is the list price in the United States $327? The PBM markup comes after the list price is set. Is the list price not the manufacturer's decision?

### [10:33 AM] DR. THORNTON:

Senator, that is a fair question, and I appreciate your engagement on the PBM issue through the HELP Committee as well. The list price, or wholesale acquisition cost, is set in a complex negotiation environment. Manufacturers set a list price knowing that PBMs and insurers will negotiate significant rebates, often 50 to 70 percent off list. The net price, what the manufacturer actually receives, is substantially lower. In 2025, the average net price for a vial of insulin was approximately $137, not $327.

### [10:35 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

But the patient at the pharmacy pays based on the list price until they meet their deductible, correct? Ms. Santos was not paying $137. She was paying $327.

### [10:35 AM] DR. THORNTON:

That is correct, and that is precisely the structural problem we believe should be addressed through PBM reform rather than manufacturer price caps.

### [10:37 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

Dr. Thornton, I appreciate your testimony, and I want to engage with the R&D argument because it is one I have made myself many times. You cited $2.1 billion as the average development cost. Can you tell me specifically how much has been invested in insulin R&D in the last ten years by the three major manufacturers?

### [10:38 AM] DR. THORNTON:

Senator, I would need to provide those specific figures for the record. The $2.1 billion figure is an industry-wide average across all therapeutic areas.

### [10:38 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

I will look forward to those figures. Because I have seen analysis from the Georgetown Health Policy Institute, and Dr. Kimball may want to speak to this, suggesting that insulin-specific R&D spending is a fraction of the revenue generated. If that is the case, the R&D justification for current pricing weakens considerably.

Let me also ask Ms. Santos: you mentioned that your insurance reclassified insulin as a specialty drug. Is that a common practice?

### [10:40 AM] MARIA SANTOS:

Senator, yes. The Insulin Affordability Coalition has documented that 23 major insurance plans reclassified at least one insulin formulation as a specialty drug between 2023 and 2025. This shifts costs directly to patients.

### [10:41 AM] SENATOR NAKAMURA:

That is concerning. I remain cautious about the price cap mechanism in S.1847, but I want to work with the Chairwoman to explore whether there is a version of this legislation that addresses the formulary classification issue as well. I think there may be a bipartisan path here.

### [10:42 AM] CHAIRWOMAN WHITFIELD:

I welcome that conversation, Senator Nakamura. My office has been in contact with several Republican members about exactly that kind of refinement.
